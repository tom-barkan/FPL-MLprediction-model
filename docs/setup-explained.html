FPL Points Predictor

A plain-English guide to everything we built — from setting up the tools to training AI models that predict Fantasy Premier League points

Setup Data Pipeline XGBoost LLM Results

The Big Picture

We built a system that predicts how many Fantasy Premier League points a player will score each gameweek. We tried two completely different AI approaches and compared them head-to-head:

  • XGBoost — a traditional "number-crunching" AI that learns from spreadsheets of stats
  • Fine-Tuned LLM — a ChatGPT-like language model that we taught to understand FPL by reading player descriptions
Think of it like cooking: We set up a kitchen (installed tools), bought ingredients (downloaded player data), then cooked the same meal using two totally different recipes to see which tastes better.

Phase 1

Building the Data Pipeline

Downloading 825 players' stats from the FPL website, cleaning the data, and preparing it for both AI models
1

Downloaded All the Data

825 players, every fixture, every gameweek — straight from the FPL website

The Fantasy Premier League website has a free, public API (a way for code to request data). We wrote a script that asked it for three things:

  1. Player profiles — name, team, position, price for all 825 players
  2. Fixtures — every match in the season with scores and difficulty ratings
  3. Player histories — gameweek-by-gameweek stats for each player (points, goals, assists, minutes, bonus, etc.)

The player histories took ~15 minutes to download because we made 825 individual requests (one per player), with a polite 1-second pause between each so we wouldn't overload the website.

Real-world analogy: Imagine going to a library and photocopying every Premier League player's report card, one at a time. It takes a while, but now you have everything in your own filing cabinet.
2

Engineered 20 Features

Turning raw data into meaningful patterns the AI can learn from

"Feature engineering" means creating useful numbers from raw data. Instead of giving the AI a pile of raw stats, we calculated things like:

3-GW form average 5-GW form average Goals per 90 min Assists per 90 min ICT index (3 GW) Minutes average Clean sheet % Bonus average Fixture difficulty Home or away Team goals scored Team goals conceded Opponent goals conceded Player price Position
Critical rule: no cheating! Every rolling average only uses past gameweeks. When predicting GW 15, the model can only see GWs 1–14. This prevents "data leakage" — the AI equivalent of reading the answers before taking the test.

Final dataset: 8,468 rows (one per player-per-gameweek), 20 columns each. We used GW 4–30 for training and GW 31+ for testing.

Real-world analogy: Raw data is like a pile of receipts. Feature engineering is like organising them into a spreadsheet with columns for "average weekly spend," "most common store," and "spending trend." Much more useful for spotting patterns.
3

Created Training Prompts for the Language Model

Converting spreadsheet data into natural language descriptions

XGBoost can read spreadsheets directly. But a language model needs words. So we converted each row of stats into a natural language description, like this:

SYSTEM
You are an FPL points predictor. Given player stats and fixture info, predict the total FPL points for the gameweek. Respond with only a single integer.

USER
Player: Salah (MID)
Gameweek: 15
Fixture: Home (FDR: 2)
Form (last 3 GW avg): 8.3 pts
Goals per 90 (season): 0.62
ICT index (last 3 GW avg): 12.1
Team goals scored (last 3 GW): 9
Price: 13.2

ASSISTANT
8

We generated 8,225 training prompts and 243 test prompts in this format. The model learns by seeing thousands of these examples and figuring out the relationship between the stats and the points.

Phase 2

Training XGBoost

The traditional "number-crunching" approach — feeding a spreadsheet of stats into a proven machine learning model
1

What is XGBoost?

A really smart spreadsheet formula

XGBoost is one of the most popular AI methods for making predictions from tables of numbers. It works by building hundreds of simple decision trees (like flowcharts) and combining their answers.

Real-world analogy: Imagine asking 500 different football pundits to predict a player's score, where each pundit specialises in one thing (form, fixtures, position, etc.). XGBoost combines all their opinions into one smart prediction. Each subsequent pundit focuses on correcting what the previous ones got wrong.

We fed it our 8,225 training rows (GW 4–30) and let it learn patterns like "midfielders in form, playing at home against weak teams, tend to score more points."

2

Results: MAE 2.11

On average, predictions are off by about 2 points

We tested on 243 player-gameweek cases from GW 31 (data the model never saw during training). Here's how it did:

Metric Score What it means
MAE 2.11 On average, predictions are off by 2.11 points
RMSE 2.81 Big misses are penalised more heavily — still reasonable
Within ±3 82.3% 4 out of 5 predictions are within 3 points of reality

By position: Best at predicting goalkeepers (MAE 1.72) and midfielders (1.83). Worst at forwards (2.64) — strikers are volatile because a single goal massively swings their score.

Is 2.11 good? Consider that the average FPL player scores about 3 points per gameweek. Being off by 2 points means the model captures the general trend but can't reliably predict hauls or blanks. This is a strong baseline to beat.
Phase 3

Fine-Tuning a Language Model

Teaching a ChatGPT-like AI to predict FPL points — running entirely on your Mac, no cloud needed
1

Downloaded the Base Model

A 2GB open-source AI brain from Hugging Face

We downloaded Llama 3.2 3B — a free, open-source language model made by Meta (the Facebook company). It's similar to ChatGPT but smaller and designed to run on personal computers.

  • "3B" = 3 billion parameters (think of these as the "brain cells" of the AI)
  • "4-bit" = compressed to use less memory, so it fits on your Mac
  • "Instruct" = already trained to follow instructions and answer questions

At this stage, the model knows nothing about FPL. It's a general-purpose AI that can chat about anything. We need to teach it.

Real-world analogy: We hired a very smart employee who speaks great English and knows about many topics — but has never heard of Fantasy Premier League. Next step: training them.
2

Fine-Tuned It with LoRA

600 training iterations on your Mac's M4 chip

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is a clever technique for teaching an AI model new skills without retraining the entire thing. Instead of adjusting all 3 billion parameters (expensive and slow), it makes small, targeted tweaks — like putting on a specialised pair of glasses.

We fed it our 7,322 training prompts and let it learn for 600 iterations. The whole process took about 20 minutes and ran entirely on your Mac using Apple's MLX framework — no expensive cloud GPU needed.

What we ran
python -m mlx_lm lora \
  --model models/llama-3.2-3b \
  --data data/mlx \
  --train --iters 600 \
  --adapter-path models/fpl-lora-adapter
Real-world analogy: Instead of sending our employee to a 4-year university degree (retraining from scratch), we sent them on a focused 2-week course specifically about FPL stats. Much faster, much cheaper, surprisingly effective.
3

Tested 4 Different Strategies

How much does fine-tuning actually help?

To understand the value of fine-tuning, we tested the same model in 4 different ways:

Zero-Shot

MAE: 9.72

Just ask the base model to predict, with no examples or training. Like asking someone who's never watched football.

Few-Shot

MAE: 2.15

Show the model 3 example predictions first, then ask it to predict. Like giving someone a quick demo before their first day.

Chain-of-Thought

MAE: 2.20

Ask the model to "think step by step" before answering. Like asking someone to show their working on a maths exam.

Fine-Tuned

MAE: 1.96

Our custom-trained model. Saw 7,322 training examples and learned the patterns. The clear winner.

Key takeaway: Zero-shot is terrible (9.72 MAE) — the model just guesses. Few-shot and chain-of-thought are decent, getting close to XGBoost with no training. But fine-tuning wins: the investment in 20 minutes of training pays off with the best score of all.

What Does This Tell Us?

The nuanced takeaways behind the numbers
  • Fine-tuning works. The fine-tuned LLM has the best MAE (1.96) and is most accurate within ±1 point (63%). Teaching the model about FPL clearly paid off.
  • XGBoost is more consistent. Its RMSE (2.81 vs 3.29) and ±3 accuracy (82.3% vs 77.8%) are better, meaning it makes fewer extreme mistakes. It's the "safe bet."
  • XGBoost is 136x faster. Under 1 second vs 2+ minutes. For real-time use (a live dashboard), XGBoost wins hands down.
  • You don't always need fine-tuning. Few-shot (just showing 3 examples) gets close to XGBoost with zero training. For quick experiments, this is powerful.
  • Zero-shot is useless. Without any FPL context, the model just guesses random numbers. Generic AI isn't enough for specialised tasks.
The bottom line: If you need the most accurate predictions overall, the fine-tuned LLM wins. If you need speed, consistency, and simplicity, XGBoost wins. In production, you'd probably want XGBoost for the dashboard and the LLM for deeper analysis.

Jargon Buster

API (Application Programming Interface)
A way for code to request data from a website. The FPL API lets our script ask "give me all player stats" and get structured data back.
Feature Engineering
The art of turning raw data into meaningful numbers. Instead of "goals in GW12" and "goals in GW13," you create "goals per 90 minutes this season" — a much more useful pattern.
MAE (Mean Absolute Error)
The average difference between predicted and actual values, ignoring direction. MAE of 2 means predictions are off by 2 points on average.
RMSE (Root Mean Square Error)
Like MAE, but it punishes big mistakes more heavily. A model with low MAE but high RMSE makes occasional huge errors.
XGBoost
A popular AI method for tabular data. It builds hundreds of decision trees (flowcharts), each one learning from the mistakes of the previous ones. Think of it as a committee of 500 pundits reaching consensus.
LLM (Large Language Model)
An AI that understands and generates human language (like ChatGPT). We used one called Llama 3.2, made by Meta.
Fine-Tuning
Teaching a pre-built AI model something new with your own data. Like sending a smart employee on a specialised training course.
LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
A technique for fine-tuning efficiently. Instead of retraining the whole model (expensive), it makes small, targeted adjustments — like hemming trousers instead of sewing a new pair.
MLX
Apple's AI engine for Mac M-series chips. It lets you run and train AI models locally instead of paying for cloud GPUs. This is what makes the whole project possible on a Mac Mini.
Zero-Shot / Few-Shot / Chain-of-Thought
Zero-shot: ask the AI with no examples. Few-shot: show it a few examples first. Chain-of-thought: ask it to think step-by-step. These are prompt engineering strategies — ways to get better results without any training.
ICT Index
An FPL metric combining Influence, Creativity, and Threat. Higher ICT = more likely to score points. Think of it as a "danger rating" for each player.
FDR (Fixture Difficulty Rating)
FPL's rating of how hard a match is, from 1 (easiest) to 5 (hardest). Playing at home against a bottom team = FDR 2. Away at Manchester City = FDR 5.
