The Big Picture

We built a system that predicts how many Fantasy Premier League points a player will score each gameweek. We tried two completely different AI approaches and compared them head-to-head:

  • XGBoost — a traditional "number-crunching" AI that learns from spreadsheets of stats
  • Fine-Tuned LLM — a ChatGPT-like language model that we taught to understand FPL by reading player descriptions
Think of it like cooking: We set up a kitchen (installed tools), bought ingredients (downloaded player data), then cooked the same meal using two totally different recipes to see which tastes better.

Phase 1

Building the Data Pipeline

Downloading 825 players' stats from the FPL website, cleaning the data, and preparing it for both AI models
1

Downloaded All the Data

825 players, every fixture, every gameweek — straight from the FPL website

The Fantasy Premier League website has a free, public API (a way for code to request data). We wrote a script that asked it for three things:

  1. Player profiles — name, team, position, price for all 825 players
  2. Fixtures — every match in the season with scores and difficulty ratings
  3. Player histories — gameweek-by-gameweek stats for each player (points, goals, assists, minutes, bonus, etc.)

The player histories took ~15 minutes to download because we made 825 individual requests (one per player), with a polite 1-second pause between each so we wouldn't overload the website.

Real-world analogy: Imagine going to a library and photocopying every Premier League player's report card, one at a time. It takes a while, but now you have everything in your own filing cabinet.
2

Engineered 20 Features

Turning raw data into meaningful patterns the AI can learn from

"Feature engineering" means creating useful numbers from raw data. Instead of giving the AI a pile of raw stats, we calculated things like:

3-GW form average 5-GW form average Goals per 90 min Assists per 90 min ICT index (3 GW) Minutes average Clean sheet % Bonus average Fixture difficulty Home or away Team goals scored Team goals conceded Opponent goals conceded Player price Position
Critical rule: no cheating! Every rolling average only uses past gameweeks. When predicting GW 15, the model can only see GWs 1–14. This prevents "data leakage" — the AI equivalent of reading the answers before taking the test.

Final dataset: 8,468 rows (one per player-per-gameweek), 20 columns each. We used GW 4–30 for training and GW 31+ for testing.

Real-world analogy: Raw data is like a pile of receipts. Feature engineering is like organising them into a spreadsheet with columns for "average weekly spend," "most common store," and "spending trend." Much more useful for spotting patterns.
3

Created Training Prompts for the Language Model

Converting spreadsheet data into natural language descriptions

XGBoost can read spreadsheets directly. But a language model needs words. So we converted each row of stats into a natural language description, like this:

SYSTEM
You are an FPL points predictor. Given player stats and fixture info, predict the total FPL points for the gameweek. Respond with only a single integer.

USER

Gameweek: 15

ASSISTANT
8

We generated 8,225 training prompts and 243 test prompts in this format. The model learns by seeing thousands of these examples and figuring out the relationship between the stats and the points.

Phase 2

Training XGBoost

The traditional "number-crunching" approach — feeding a spreadsheet of stats into a proven machine learning model
1

What is XGBoost?

A really smart spreadsheet formula

XGBoost is one of the most popular AI methods for making predictions from tables of numbers. It works by building hundreds of simple decision trees (like flowcharts) and combining their answers.

Real-world analogy: Imagine asking 500 different football pundits to predict a player's score, where each pundit specialises in one thing (form, fixtures, position, etc.). XGBoost combines all their opinions into one smart prediction. Each subsequent pundit focuses on correcting what the previous ones got wrong.

We fed it our 8,225 training rows (GW 4–30) and let it learn patterns like "midfielders in form, playing at home against weak teams, tend to score more points."

2

Results: MAE 2.11

On average, predictions are off by about 2 points

We tested on 243 player-gameweek cases from GW 31 (data the model never saw during training). Here's how it did:

Metric Score What it means
MAE 2.11 On average, predictions are off by 2.11 points
RMSE 2.81 Big misses are penalised more heavily — still reasonable
Within ±3 82.3% 4 out of 5 predictions are within 3 points of reality

By position: Best at predicting goalkeepers (MAE 1.72) and midfielders (1.83). Worst at forwards (2.64) — strikers are volatile because a single goal massively swings their score.

Is 2.11 good? Consider that the average FPL player scores about 3 points per gameweek. Being off by 2 points means the model captures the general trend but can't reliably predict hauls or blanks. This is a strong baseline to beat.
Phase 3

Fine-Tuning a Language Model

Teaching a ChatGPT-like AI to predict FPL points — running entirely on your Mac, no cloud needed
1

Downloaded the Base Model

A 2GB open-source AI brain from Hugging Face

We downloaded Llama 3.2 3B — a free, open-source language model made by Meta (the Facebook company). It's similar to ChatGPT but smaller and designed to run on personal computers.

  • "3B" = 3 billion parameters (think of these as the "brain cells" of the AI)
  • "4-bit" = compressed to use less memory, so it fits on your Mac
  • "Instruct" = already trained to follow instructions and answer questions

At this stage, the model knows nothing about FPL. It's a general-purpose AI that can chat about anything. We need to teach it.

Real-world analogy: We hired a very smart employee who speaks great English and knows about many topics — but has never heard of Fantasy Premier League. Next step: training them.
2

Fine-Tuned It with LoRA

Our first attempt — 600 training iterations on your Mac's M4 chip

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is a clever technique for teaching an AI model new skills without retraining the entire thing. Instead of adjusting all 3 billion parameters (expensive and slow), it makes small, targeted tweaks — like putting on a specialised pair of glasses.

We fed it our 7,322 training prompts and let it learn for 600 iterations. The whole process took about 20 minutes and ran entirely on your Mac using Apple's MLX framework — no expensive cloud GPU needed. (This was v1 — we'd later discover it was broken and iterate twice more. See the iteration journey.)

What we ran
python -m mlx_lm lora \
  --model models/llama-3.2-3b \
  --data data/mlx \
  --train --iters 600 \
  --adapter-path models/fpl-lora-adapter
Real-world analogy: Instead of sending our employee to a 4-year university degree (retraining from scratch), we sent them on a focused 2-week course specifically about FPL stats. Much faster, much cheaper, surprisingly effective.
3

Tested 4 Different Strategies

How much does fine-tuning actually help?

To understand the value of fine-tuning, we tested the same model in 4 different ways:

Zero-Shot

MAE: 11.54

Just ask the base model to predict, with no examples or training. Like asking someone who's never watched football.

Few-Shot

MAE: 2.16

Show the model 3 example predictions first, then ask it to predict. Like giving someone a quick demo before their first day.

Chain-of-Thought

MAE: 2.20

Ask the model to "think step by step" before answering. Like asking someone to show their working on a maths exam.

Fine-Tuned (v3)

MAE: 3.35

Our custom-trained model (3 iterations). Higher MAE because it predicts across the full 0–10 range — bigger misses, but better player ranking.

Surprise finding: Few-shot (just 3 examples in the prompt, no training) nearly matches XGBoost (2.16 vs 2.11). Fine-tuning trades raw accuracy for differentiation — it's the only approach that predicts high-value hauls (9–10 pts). More on this in the iteration journey below.

What Does This Tell Us?

The nuanced takeaways behind the numbers
  • XGBoost wins on accuracy. Lowest MAE (2.11), lowest RMSE (2.81), and highest ±3 accuracy (82.3%). It's fast, consistent, and the clear winner on raw prediction quality.
  • Few-shot is shockingly close. Just 3 examples in the prompt — no training at all — gets MAE 2.16. That's within 0.05 of XGBoost's score. For quick experiments, this is a revelation.
  • Fine-tuned v3 trades accuracy for range. Its MAE (3.35) is higher because it predicts across the full 0–10 spectrum. Bigger misses when wrong, but it's the only approach that identifies potential 9–10 point hauls. Better for ranking players than predicting exact scores.
  • XGBoost is 370x faster. Under 1 second vs 6+ minutes. For a live dashboard, there's no contest.
  • Zero-shot is useless. Without any FPL context, the model just guesses random numbers (MAE 11.54). Generic AI isn't enough for specialised tasks.
The bottom line: XGBoost wins on accuracy and speed. But the interesting story is why the fine-tuned LLM's numbers changed across 3 iterations — and what that teaches us about training AI models. Read on.
The Iteration Journey

3 Attempts to Fix the LLM

Fine-tuning took 3 iterations to get right. Each failure taught us something about how AI models learn — and where they take shortcuts.
1

The Problem: Mode Collapse

The AI found a cheat code — and it backfired

When we pointed our fine-tuned LLM at 344 real players to predict their next gameweek, something odd happened: every single player got the same prediction — 2 points. Mo Salah at home against a relegation side? 2 points. A backup defender away at City? 2 points.

What went wrong: The model discovered that "2" is the safest answer. In FPL, 2 points is the most common score (you get 2 just for playing 60+ minutes). By always saying "2," the model was technically less wrong on average than if it tried to predict actual highs and lows. It optimised for being "least wrong" instead of being "most useful."

This is called "mode collapse" — the AI collapses to always predicting the single most common outcome. It's one of the most common problems in AI, and it happens because:

Real-world analogy: Imagine a weather forecaster in London who discovers that saying "cloudy" every day is correct 60% of the time. They'll have a great overall accuracy score — but they'll never warn you about storms or promise you sunshine. Technically accurate, practically useless.
2

The Root Cause: Lopsided Training Data

When 58% of your examples look the same, the AI learns to always give the same answer

We looked at our training data and the problem was obvious. Here's what the model was learning from:

We also made the model more capable: 16 LoRA layers (up from 8), 3x the learning rate, and 500 training iterations. This gave the model more capacity to learn the nuances between different point values.

Production Pipeline

Smoothing: From Discrete to Continuous

The v3 model predicts 6 integers. For the dashboard, we blend it with player features to produce realistic decimal predictions.
1

The Problem with Raw LLM Output

6 integers aren't enough for a useful dashboard

The fine-tuned v3 model predicts one of {0, 1, 2, 4, 9, 10} for every player. That's great for ranking (Salah gets 10, a bench warmer gets 0), but it means:

  • 150+ players all get the same prediction of "2"
  • No way to distinguish between a "strong 2" and a "weak 2"
  • The dashboard can't rank players within the same bracket
Think of it like this: Imagine a restaurant reviewer who only gives 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 stars. That's useful for broad rankings, but it can't tell you whether a 4-star restaurant is "almost 5" or "barely 4." You need decimal scores for finer ordering.
2

The Smoothing Formula

40% LLM signal + 30% recent form + 30% feature-based estimate

We blend three signals to produce realistic decimal predictions:

Step 1: Dampen LLM signal
  llm_dampened = raw_prediction × 0.6 + 1.0  (clip to 0.5–7.5)

Step 2: Build a feature-based estimate
  feature_est = form × 0.5 + form_5gw × 0.3 + ict × 0.15
    + fixture_adjustment + attack_bonus + clean_sheet_bonus
    × minutes_factor

Step 3: Blend all three components
  final = 40% × llm_dampened + 30% × recent_form + 30% × feature_est

Step 4: Clip to 0.5–10.0, round to 1 decimal place

Why blend? The LLM provides directional signal (who will score high vs low), while form stats provide calibration (realistic point ranges). Blending captures both — the LLM's pattern recognition and the stats' grounding in reality.
3

Confidence Calculation

Not all predictions are equally reliable

Each LLM prediction comes with a confidence score based on four heuristics:

  • Form consistency — do the 3-game and 5-game averages agree? Consistent form = more predictable
  • Minutes played — a guaranteed starter (90 mins) is more predictable than a rotation risk (45 mins)
  • Fixture clarity — extreme fixtures (FDR 1 or 5) are easier to predict than mid-table matchups (FDR 3)
  • Prediction-form alignment — does the prediction match the player's recent output?

The dashboard's combined value score uses both models: (XGB pts × XGB confidence + LLM pts × LLM confidence) / 2. Players where both models agree and predict high points rise to the top.

What We Learned

Key Lessons from the Experiment

The most valuable insights aren't just the final numbers — they're the surprises we found along the way

Lesson 1: Few-Shot Prompting Nearly Matches XGBoost

The most unexpected result of the entire project

Giving the base model (no fine-tuning) just 3 examples in the prompt achieved MAE 2.16 — within 0.05 of XGBoost's 2.11. No training, no data balancing, no LoRA. Just a well-written prompt.

XGBoost
2.11
Few-Shot (0 training)
2.16
Implication: For many prediction tasks, prompt engineering can get you 97% of the way there with zero infrastructure investment. The remaining 3% requires a full ML pipeline. Whether that last 3% is worth it depends entirely on your use case.

Lesson 2: Data Quality > Model Capacity

The most impactful change was fixing the training data, not making the model bigger

Across our 3 iterations, the biggest leap came from balancing the training data (v1 → v2), not from doubling the LoRA layers (v2 → v3):

  • v1 → v2 (data fix): Went from 1 unique prediction to 4. The model finally learned to differentiate.
  • v2 → v3 (data + capacity): Went from 4 unique predictions to 6. The model learned the full range.

Doubling LoRA layers without fixing the data would have just produced a more confident "always predict 2" model. The data was the bottleneck.

The takeaway for any AI project: Before making your model bigger, look at your training data. Is it balanced? Is it representative? Fixing data problems is almost always higher-leverage than scaling model parameters.

Lesson 3: Aggregate Metrics Can Hide Failures

MAE looked fine. The predictions were useless.

The v1 model (always predicting "2") had a decent-looking MAE — because 2 points is close to the average FPL score. If we'd only checked MAE, we might have shipped a model that was technically accurate but practically useless.

We only caught the problem by inspecting individual predictions and noticing they were all identical. This led us to check the prediction distribution, not just the aggregate error.

Rule of thumb: Always check the distribution of your model's outputs, not just the average error. A model that predicts the mean for every input will have a low MAE but zero utility. Look at unique values, histograms, and individual edge cases.

Lesson 4: Accuracy vs Differentiation Trade-Off

The "best" model depends on what you're using it for

The fine-tuned v3 model has worse MAE than XGBoost (3.35 vs 2.11). But it provides something XGBoost doesn't: meaningful differentiation at the extremes.

  • If you need exact point predictions: XGBoost wins (lower MAE, tighter error bounds)
  • If you need to rank players: Fine-tuned v3 is more useful (identifies potential 9–10 pt hauls)
  • If you need quick, good-enough predictions: Few-shot prompting gets you 97% of XGBoost with zero training
Think of it like hiring scouts: XGBoost is the precise scout who rates every player 5.2 to 7.8 (accurate but narrow). The fine-tuned LLM is the scout who says "this player is a 10, that one is a 0" (sometimes wrong, but identifies the standouts). You want both perspectives.

3

How Confidence Is Calculated

Not all predictions are created equal

Each model calculates confidence differently:

  • XGBoost confidence is based on how much its internal "committee of decision trees" agrees. When all the trees give similar answers, confidence is high. When they disagree, it drops.
  • LLM confidence is based on the input features: consistent recent form (3-game and 5-game averages align), high minutes (the player is a guaranteed starter), clear fixture difficulty (very easy or very hard — mid-table opponents are hardest to predict), and whether the prediction aligns with recent form.

The combined value score averages both: (XGB pts × XGB confidence + LLM pts × LLM confidence) / 2. This rewards players where both models are confident and predict high points.

Jargon Buster

API (Application Programming Interface)
A way for code to request data from a website. The FPL API lets our script ask "give me all player stats" and get structured data back.
Feature Engineering
The art of turning raw data into meaningful numbers. Instead of "goals in GW12" and "goals in GW13," you create "goals per 90 minutes this season" — a much more useful pattern.
MAE (Mean Absolute Error)
The average difference between predicted and actual values, ignoring direction. MAE of 2 means predictions are off by 2 points on average.
RMSE (Root Mean Square Error)
Like MAE, but it punishes big mistakes more heavily. A model with low MAE but high RMSE makes occasional huge errors.
XGBoost
A popular AI method for tabular data. It builds hundreds of decision trees (flowcharts), each one learning from the mistakes of the previous ones. Think of it as a committee of 500 pundits reaching consensus.
LLM (Large Language Model)
An AI that understands and generates human language (like ChatGPT). We used one called Llama 3.2, made by Meta.
Fine-Tuning
Teaching a pre-built AI model something new with your own data. Like sending a smart employee on a specialised training course.
LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
A technique for fine-tuning efficiently. Instead of retraining the whole model (expensive), it makes small, targeted adjustments — like hemming trousers instead of sewing a new pair.
MLX
Apple's AI engine for Mac M-series chips. It lets you run and train AI models locally instead of paying for cloud GPUs. This is what makes the whole project possible on a Mac Mini.
Zero-Shot / Few-Shot / Chain-of-Thought
Zero-shot: ask the AI with no examples. Few-shot: show it a few examples first. Chain-of-thought: ask it to think step-by-step. These are prompt engineering strategies — ways to get better results without any training.
Mode Collapse
When an AI model learns to always give the same answer because that one answer was the most common in its training data. Like a student who discovers "B" is the most common answer on multiple choice tests and just circles B every time. Technically gets the best average score, but useless in practice.
Data Balancing / Oversampling
Adjusting training data so the AI sees roughly equal numbers of each type of outcome. If 60% of examples are "2 points," the model only learns to say "2." By making the dataset more balanced, the model is forced to learn the actual patterns instead of taking shortcuts.
ICT Index
An FPL metric combining Influence, Creativity, and Threat. Higher ICT = more likely to score points. Think of it as a "danger rating" for each player.
FDR (Fixture Difficulty Rating)
FPL's rating of how hard a match is, from 1 (easiest) to 5 (hardest). Playing at home against a bottom team = FDR 2. Away at Manchester City = FDR 5.
